# Cuemath Brand Awareness Campaign 2026
## "Thinkers, Not Calculators" — Strategy & Content Deck

**Prepared:** April 2026
**Channels:** Meta (Instagram/Facebook) + YouTube
**Duration:** 12 months (May 2026 – April 2027)
**Monthly output:** 7–8 pieces across 6 recurring series + quarterly anchors
**Annual output:** ~90 pieces

---

## 1. Why Now — The Strategic Context

Cuemath has been in market for 12 years. 200K+ students. 10K reviews at 4.9 on Trustpilot. NRI-specific lead gen ads running for 3–4 years with NRI filters. In the US NRI community, most parents have *heard* of Cuemath.

**The problem isn't awareness. It's categorization.**

Right now, a typical NRI parent buckets Cuemath alongside Kumon, Mathnasium, local tutors, and random online options — all in one mental category: "math tutoring." When they think about getting help for their child's math, Cuemath is one of many options on a list.

**The job of this campaign: Move Cuemath off that list entirely.**

Create a new category — MathFit™ — that only Cuemath occupies. Make every other option feel like "just tutoring" by contrast. When a parent in this community thinks about their child's mathematical future, the first word that comes to mind should be MathFit™, and the only brand attached to it should be Cuemath.

**Success looks like:** An NRI parent at a dinner party saying, "We don't do tutoring. We do Cuemath — it's different. They actually teach kids to think."

---

## 2. The One Belief

Every single piece of content — all 90+ over the year — ladders to one idea:

> **"Cuemath doesn't teach math. They teach kids how to think."**

This is the belief that creates the category. It separates Cuemath from:
- Kumon (drill-based, worksheet repetition)
- Mathnasium (center-based, grade remediation)
- Private tutors (homework help, test prep)
- Online platforms (self-paced, no real teaching)

None of them can credibly claim "we teach thinking" because their pedagogy doesn't support it. Cuemath's does — the tutor training, the Cuemath Way ("cue don't tell"), the MathFit™ framework. This isn't marketing language. It's a genuine differentiator turned into a brand position.

## 3. Audience Definition

### Primary: The NRI Researcher Mother
- 35–44, Indian diaspora in US/AUS/UK/MEA
- Already aware Cuemath exists (exposed to 3–4 years of lead gen ads)
- Currently categorizes Cuemath as "one of several math tutoring options"
- Researches deeply before committing; active in parent WhatsApp groups, school communities
- Child is mostly "average or at par" (54.3%) or "very good" (42.3%) — enrichment buyer, not remediation

### Secondary: The Adjacent Non-NRI Parent
- Encounters Cuemath for the first time through this content
- Lower intent but the belief-led content does the job of explanation + differentiation simultaneously
- The "thinking vs. calculating" frame is universally resonant, not culturally specific

### Anti-Audience: The Shortcut Seeker
- Wants guaranteed grades, fast results, cheapest option
- This content should actively repel them — that's a feature, not a bug
- Every parent who self-selects out saves acquisition cost and protects retention

### Series 1: "MathFit™ vs. Math Done"
**Role:** The category definer. The single most important series.
**Frequency:** 2x/month (24 pieces/year)
**Length:** 30–45 seconds
**Channels:** Meta Reels (primary), YouTube Shorts (secondary)

Split-screen or sequential contrast. Same math concept, two completely different outcomes. One child "does" the math — gets the right answer, moves on. The other child is MathFit™ — understands why, can explain it, applies it to the real world.

No voiceover. Minimal text supers. Let the visual contrast do the work.

### Series 2: "The Real Story"
**Role:** Emotional proof. The series that makes parents *feel* the transformation.
**Frequency:** 1x/month (12 pieces/year)
**Length:** 60–90 seconds
**Channels:** Meta Feed (primary), YouTube In-stream (secondary)

#### The Concept
Real Cuemath parents (or faithful recreations) telling their story in a docu-style format. Not a product review. A *belief* testimonial — narrating the moment their thinking about math education changed, and what happened after.

#### The Story Arc (Same structure, every episode)

**Act 1 — "Everyone told me..." (15–20s)**
The default advice from the world. What society, school, other parents told them about math education.
- "Everyone said just get a tutor."
- "My friends said Kumon worked for their kids."
- "The school said he's fine — he's passing."

**Act 2 — "But I noticed..." (15–20s)**
The private doubt. The thing only a parent paying close attention would see.
- "But I noticed she could solve the problems in the textbook... and completely fall apart with anything different."
- "He was getting A's but he couldn't explain what he was learning. Not to me, not to his little sister, not to anyone."
- "She'd memorize before every test and forget everything after. I kept thinking — what's the point of this cycle?"

**Act 3 — "So I looked for something different..." (10–15s)**
Not "I found Cuemath" — the search itself. The moment of choosing a different philosophy.
- "I didn't want another tutor who'd just do homework with him. I wanted someone who'd change how he thinks about math."
- "I started reading about conceptual learning vs. procedural learning. That's when the name kept coming up."

**Act 4 — "And now..." (15–20s)**
The transformation. NOT grades or scores. A *moment* of thinking.
- "Last week she explained negative numbers to her 6-year-old cousin using a number line she drew herself. Nobody asked her to."
- "He called me from the car and said 'Mom, I figured out why that math trick works.' He was excited. About math."
- "Her teacher pulled me aside and said 'whatever you're doing at home, keep doing it. She's asking different kinds of questions now.'"

**End frame (5s):** *Cuemath. Making kids MathFit™.*

#### Content Variations by Market

**US NRI parents (6 of the 12):**
- Foundation Rebuilder parent — child was "fine" but not understanding
- Competition parent — child needed deeper thinking for AMC/Olympiad
- Multi-sibling family — second child enrolled after seeing first child's transformation
- Working mother — appreciated the structure and transparency of progress tracking
- Father perspective — "we" decision, quantitative framing
- Long-tenured parent (2+ years) — compound effect story

**Australia NRI parents (3 of the 12):**
- NAPLAN prep story — school didn't provide depth needed for top bands
- Selective school entry — gap between regular curriculum and test requirements
- Year-level advancement — child working 1–2 years ahead through Cuemath

**UK/MEA NRI parents (3 of the 12):**
- UK: 11+ / grammar school prep — reasoning skills, not just arithmetic
- MEA: International curriculum alignment — CBSE/IB student needing conceptual depth
- Universal: "We moved countries and Cuemath was the one thing that stayed consistent"

#### Production Notes
- Film in the parent's actual home. Kitchen table, living room, backyard. No studio.
- One camera, eye-level, slightly warm color grade. Intimate, not polished.
- Parent should NOT be reading a script. Share the 4-act arc as talking points, then let them talk. Edit for story, not perfection.
- B-roll of the child (studying, playing, at school) intercut — but never staged or overly cute.
- If the child has a great one-liner ("I actually like math now" or "My tutor doesn't give me the answer, she makes me find it"), use it. But don't force it.

#### Channel Notes
- **Meta Feed:** 1:1 format. These are sit-and-watch, not scroll-and-catch. Target warm audiences — people who've engaged with Series 1 or 4 previously.
- **YouTube In-stream:** 16:9, skippable. The first 5 seconds must hook — open with the most surprising parent quote from Act 2 or Act 4 (not Act 1).

**Length:** 15–30 seconds
**Channels:** Meta Reels (primary), YouTube Shorts + Bumper Ads (secondary)

Short, punchy, slightly provocative content that taps into the universal parental anxiety about AI and education. Each piece creates a sharp contrast between what AI can do and what only a thinking child can do.

These are designed for maximum reach and shareability — the content NRI parents forward to each other in WhatsApp groups and comment on publicly.

### Series 4: "The WhatsApp Clip"
**Role:** Community shareability. The content that spreads organically.
**Frequency:** 2x/month (24 pieces/year)
**Length:** 15–20 seconds
**Channels:** Meta Stories + Reels (primary), YouTube Shorts (secondary)

#### The Concept
Content designed to *look and feel* like something a parent actually shot on their phone and shared in a family WhatsApp group. Raw. Vertical. Unpolished on purpose. These don't feel like ads — they feel like moments.

The insight: In the NRI community, WhatsApp groups (school parents, extended family, community groups) are the primary content-sharing channel. Content that looks "produced" gets watched but not forwarded. Content that looks "real" gets forwarded with a personal message: "Look at this — have you seen this for your kids?"

#### Content Types

**Type A: "Look What My Kid Just Did" (8 of 24)**
Parent's phone camera. Child doing something mathematically impressive without realizing it.
- Kid explaining a math concept at the dinner table to the family. Parent films quietly.
- Kid doing mental math at a store, getting it right, looking proud.
- Kid correcting a math mistake on TV or in a YouTube video. "Wait that's wrong because..."
- Kid teaching a younger sibling using a method they learned in Cuemath.
- Kid solving a puzzle by reasoning through it, talking out loud.
- Kid asking an unexpectedly deep math question. Parent catches it on video.
- Kid reacting to getting a hard problem right after struggling with it.
- Kid explaining to a friend why a "math trick" from TikTok doesn't actually work.

**Type B: "Screen Moments" (8 of 24)**
Screen recordings of the Cuemath platform or class.
- A whiteboard moment where the child figures something out — the "ohhhh" moment.
- A tutor asking a question and patiently waiting while the child reasons through it (the anti-app moment).
- A progress report screenshot with parent's reaction voiceover: "This is 5 months."
- A side-by-side: child's school worksheet (right answers, no understanding) vs. Cuemath class (struggle, then real understanding).

**Type C: "Parent Real Talk" (8 of 24)**
Parent speaking directly to camera, selfie-style. Unscripted.
- "I want to tell you about the moment I knew this was working..."
- "Everyone asks me why I didn't just go with Kumon. Here's why."
- "My kid said something about math yesterday that made me tear up. I know that sounds dramatic but..."
- "We've been doing Cuemath for 8 months. Here's what I didn't expect."
- "Three things I wish I'd known before we started math tutoring."
- "The difference between my two kids — one before Cuemath, one after."
- "What my daughter's school teacher said about her math this semester."
- "Why I stopped comparing Cuemath to tutoring. It's not the same thing."

#### Production Notes
- **THIS SERIES MUST NOT LOOK PRODUCED.** The moment it looks like an ad, it loses all its power.
- Film on an iPhone. Vertical. Natural audio (including background noise — that's a feature).
- No color grading. No transitions. No background music.
- If using real parents: give them ONE prompt and let them go. Don't reshoot.
- If using actors: make them genuinely improv. Brief them on the scenario, not the script.
- Cuemath branding: ONLY in the last 2 seconds. A small logo and "Making kids MathFit™" super. Not a title card — a subtle text overlay.

#### Channel Notes
- **Meta Stories:** Primary. Stories are the most "forwarded" format. Add a "Send to a friend" sticker or "Share this with a parent who needs to see it" text prompt.
- **Meta Reels:** Secondary. The algorithm will pick up high-completion ones.
- **YouTube Shorts:** Repurpose the best performers from Meta. Test monthly.

### Series 5: "The Tutor Moment"
**Role:** Product differentiation. Show what makes Cuemath tutoring fundamentally different.
**Frequency:** Every other month (6 pieces/year)
**Length:** 45–60 seconds
**Channels:** Meta Feed (primary), YouTube In-stream (secondary)

#### The Concept
60.8% of Cuemath parents mention their tutor by name. The tutor is the emotional anchor of the product experience. This series captures *how* Cuemath tutors teach differently — not through claims, but through moments.

Each episode focuses on one teaching interaction — a tutor choosing to ask a question instead of giving an answer, waiting through silence instead of filling it, celebrating the struggle instead of the answer.

#### Content Executions

| # | The Moment | What It Shows |
|---|------------|---------------|
| 1 | Child gives a wrong answer. Tutor doesn't say "no." Tutor says: "Interesting — walk me through how you got there." Child traces their reasoning, finds their own mistake, corrects it. | The Cuemath Way: "cue don't tell." Understanding > correction. |
| 2 | Child is frustrated, stuck on a problem for 3 minutes. Tutor doesn't rescue them. Gives one small hint. Child's face when they crack it — that's the frame. | Productive struggle. The tutor's restraint IS the pedagogy. |
| 3 | Parent watching the class from another room. Overhears the tutor ask "why does that work?" and hears their child actually explain it. Parent's expression: surprise, then pride. | The parent realizes this isn't tutoring. This is something different. |
| 4 | Tutor pulls up a real-world scenario instead of a textbook problem. Kid leans in. This doesn't feel like homework anymore. | Relevance. Math connected to life, not just curriculum. |
| 5 | Two clips from the same child — month 1 vs. month 6. In month 1, child waits for the tutor to start. In month 6, child comes in with a question *they* want to explore. | The shift from passive to active learner. The system working. |
| 6 | Tutor and child laughing together over a creative approach to a problem that technically works but is hilariously roundabout. Then the tutor shows the elegant solution. | The relationship. It's not clinical. It's warm, personal, human. |

#### Production Notes
- Use real Cuemath class recordings where possible (with permission). Authenticity > production value.
- If recreating: cast a real Cuemath tutor. They know the pedagogy — they'll be more natural than an actor.
- Focus the camera on the child's face during the thinking moment. The "figuring it out" expression is the hero shot.
- Include the tutor's first name in the super: *"Priya, Cuemath tutor. 4 years, 200+ students."*

#### Channel Notes
- **Meta Feed:** 1:1 or 4:5. These are considered content — not scroll-stoppers. Target parents who've already engaged with Series 1 or 3.
- **YouTube In-stream:** 16:9, skippable. Pre-roll before education content. The "cue don't tell" moment in the first 5 seconds is the hook — it's surprising enough to prevent skip.

### Series 6: "In Their Own Words"
**Role:** Aspiration. The product demo disguised as kid content.
**Frequency:** Every other month, alternating with Series 5 (6 pieces/year)
**Length:** 30–60 seconds
**Channels:** YouTube In-stream (primary), Meta Feed (secondary)

#### The Concept
Real Cuemath students (ages 8–13) explaining a math concept to camera. Not performing. Not reciting. Just a kid reasoning through an idea in their own words — with all the imperfect, genuine, beautiful messiness of a child's mind working in real time.

No branding until the end. No mention of Cuemath by the child. The viewer should think "how does this kid think like this?" — and then see the answer.

#### Content Executions

| # | Concept | What the Child Explains |
|---|---------|------------------------|
| 1 | Why dividing by a fraction means multiplying by the reciprocal | Not the rule — the *reason*. Using pizzas, or sharing, or whatever metaphor makes sense to them. |
| 2 | Why negative times negative is positive | Their own logical reasoning, probably with a number line or a story. |
| 3 | What algebra really is | "It's like a mystery where the letter is the thing you're trying to find..." |
| 4 | Why you can't divide by zero | Kid tries it, hits the paradox, explains why it breaks. |
| 5 | How percentages actually work | Applied to something they care about — a sale, a game score, a batting average. |
| 6 | What infinity means | A child grappling with a genuinely hard philosophical/mathematical idea. Struggling and loving it. |

#### Production Notes
- Interview the child for 10–15 minutes. Use the best 30–60 seconds.
- Ask the question, then DON'T help. Their struggle, their language, their metaphors.
- Film at their home, sitting on a couch or at their desk. Casual. Not a classroom.
- If they use a whiteboard, paper, or objects to explain — let them. Don't art-direct it.
- The imperfection is the point. A child saying "wait, no, let me think about this again" is MORE compelling than a polished explanation.

**Super at end:** *"[Child's name], age [X]. Cuemath student for [Y] years. Making kids MathFit™."*

#### Channel Notes
- **YouTube In-stream:** Primary. These are longer-watch content. Pre-roll before parenting or education channels. The child's voice in the first 3 seconds is the hook — it's unexpected in an ad slot.
- **Meta Feed:** Secondary. 1:1 crop. These tend to get saved and shared — track save rate as the key metric.

### Series A: "The Cuemath Standard" (Quarterly Anchors)
**Role:** Brand authority. Prestige content. The tent poles the monthly content orbits around.
**Frequency:** 1x/quarter (4 pieces/year)
**Length:** 90–120 seconds
**Channels:** YouTube In-stream (primary, non-skip or TrueView), Meta Feed (secondary)

#### The Concept
Four major pieces per year that establish Cuemath's authority and depth. These are the most produced, most polished content in the system — the pieces you'd show a board member, a potential partner, or a journalist.

#### Q1: "The Top 1%"
What it actually means to be a Cuemath tutor. The selection process (what gets filtered out), the training (how they learn to "cue not tell"), the ongoing development. Featuring 2–3 real tutors talking about their philosophy.

**Key moment:** A tutor describing the hardest part of their job — "not giving the answer when I can see the child is close. Letting them struggle. That's the skill."

**Why it works:** Every tutoring company says "great tutors." This shows what Cuemath means by it — and it's fundamentally different.

#### Q2: "The Parents' Table"
A roundtable conversation — 4 real Cuemath parents from 4 different countries (US, Australia, UK, India). Unscripted. Moderated lightly. They discuss: what mathematical confidence means in their household, what they noticed change in their child, and what they'd tell a parent who's still deciding.

**Key moment:** The moment one parent says something and the others nod — the universal recognition that they're all experiencing the same transformation despite different schools, curricula, and countries.

**Why it works:** This is referral energy captured on film. The viewer feels like they're eavesdropping on the exact conversation that happens in NRI WhatsApp groups.

#### Q3: "MathFit™ for the AI Generation"
The manifesto piece. A short film built on the master frame: "AI calculates. Humans must think." Intercuts between the AI world accelerating and children thinking deeply. Not dystopian — aspirational. The message: the most future-proof skill you can give your child isn't coding. It's mathematical reasoning.

**Key moment:** A child watching AI solve a problem, then saying something like: "But it doesn't know *why* it works."

**Why it works:** This is the belief distilled into film. It positions MathFit™ as the answer to the biggest question parents have about their child's future.

#### Q4: "One Year of MathFit™"
A compilation piece. 3–4 real student journeys compressed into a 2-minute film — where they started, what changed, where they are now. Intercut with moments from throughout the year's content. The annual proof point.

**Key moment:** Parents watching their child do something mathematically confident, in real life, without prompting. The look on the parent's face.

**Why it works:** This is the emotional ROI case for Cuemath — a year of transformation made visible.

#### Production Notes
- These get real budget. Professional crew, proper editing, color grading, sound design.
- But still grounded in reality. Real people, real locations. Not a TV commercial aesthetic — more documentary premium. Think Vice/Vox style, not P&G.
- Music: original score or licensed. Minimal, tasteful. Piano/strings, not corporate synth.

#### Channel Notes
- **YouTube TrueView In-stream:** Primary. These are 90–120s so they need the skippable format — but the first 5 seconds must hook hard enough that parents choose to keep watching.
- **Meta Feed:** Cut down to 60s for Feed. Use the 90–120s version as a "See More" format.
- **Paid promotion:** These get the biggest media push of the quarter. Use as tentpole content around which monthly content clusters.

## 6. Cross-Channel Coordination

### The Flywheel

```
YouTube Bumpers (6s, AI hook)
  → builds curiosity
    → Meta Reels (30s, MathFit™ vs Math Done)
      → builds understanding of the category
        → Meta Feed / YT TrueView (60–90s, Real Stories)
          → builds emotional conviction
            → Retargeted with existing lead gen ads
              → Trial booking
```

The awareness campaign doesn't replace lead gen. It makes lead gen more efficient by pre-selling the belief. A parent who's seen 10+ awareness pieces and then encounters a "Book a free class" ad is a fundamentally different prospect than a cold lead.

### Audience Handoff
- Build a **"Brand Engaged" audience** on Meta: anyone who's watched 50%+ of any awareness content OR engaged with awareness posts
- Feed this audience into existing lead gen campaigns as a **warm targeting layer**
- Track: does CPQL/CPTD decrease for the brand-engaged audience vs. cold NRI targeting?
- This is the single most important proof point that the awareness campaign is working.

### Content Repurposing
Every piece of content should be cut for multiple formats:

| Master Format | Recut → Meta | Recut → YouTube | Recut → Organic |
|--------------|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 90s parent story | 60s Feed + 30s Reel teaser | 90s TrueView + 6s bumper (best quote) | Full version on channel + quote cards for IG |
| 45s MathFit vs Math Done | 30s Reel + 15s Story | 30s Short + 6s bumper | Still frame carousel showing the contrast |
| 30s AI hook | 15s Reel + 15s Story | 15s Short + 6s bumper | Quote card with the super text |

One production → 3–4 distribution formats. This keeps the 7–8 piece/month production volume manageable while maximizing distribution.

## 9. The Non-Negotiables

Before going into production, align on these principles:

1. **Every piece ladders to one belief.** "Cuemath teaches thinking, not just math." If a script doesn't serve this, kill it.

2. **MathFit™ is the category, not the product.** We're not selling a tutoring service. We're defining what mathematical readiness looks like — and then being the only brand that delivers it.

3. **Enrichment, always.** Never imply the child is struggling. 54.3% of Cuemath kids are "average or at par," 42.3% are "very good." This is about going deeper, not catching up.

4. **The mother is the audience.** She's the researcher, the decision-maker. Speak to her intelligence, her instinct, her standards. Never talk down.

5. **Authenticity > production value.** A real parent stumbling through a genuine story is 10x more powerful than a polished actor delivering a perfect script. Especially for Series 2 and 4.

6. **Brand reveals late.** In most series, Cuemath doesn't appear until the last 3–5 seconds. The content should be valuable even without the brand. That's what makes it shareable.

7. **Repel the wrong audience.** Content that attracts shortcut seekers wastes budget and hurts retention. The understanding-first, thinking-first framing is a deliberate filter.

8. **Connect to lead gen.** This campaign doesn't exist in isolation. Every exposed audience should flow into performance campaigns. Track the handoff relentlessly.
